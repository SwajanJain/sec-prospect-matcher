## 1. Who Is the ICP?

### The Organization

**Nonprofit advancement offices** — the teams at schools, universities, hospitals, and large nonprofits responsible for raising money from donors.

"Advancement" is the umbrella term covering fundraising (development), alumni relations, prospect research, advancement services (data/analytics/gift processing), and donor communications. Every institution with an endowment or fundraising program has one.

**Size of the market:**
- ~4,000 colleges and universities in the US (most have development offices)
- ~34,000 private K-12 schools (elite schools like Phillips Academy have dedicated research staff)
- ~6,000+ hospitals and health systems with foundation/development arms
- ~1,000+ large national nonprofits with dedicated prospect research
- **Conservative TAM:** 5,000-10,000 organizations with dedicated prospect research
- **Broader TAM:** 20,000-40,000 organizations doing some form of systematic prospect research

**Money in motion:**
- **$592.5 billion** in total US charitable giving in 2024 (up 6.3% YoY)
- **$88 billion** to education specifically (up 13.2%, an all-time high)
- Fewer donors are giving, but total amounts are steady — meaning major donor identification is more critical than ever

### The Three Personas

#### Persona 1: The Buyer — Director of Prospect Development / Research Manager

This is who makes the purchase decision.

- **Team size they manage:** 1-10 people. Often just 2-3 at mid-tier universities.
- **Reports to:** VP of Advancement/Development
- **Controls:** Vendor budget for research tools
- **Evaluates:** Whether the tool fits alongside or replaces existing tools (iWave, DonorSearch, Google Alerts)
- **Total addressable audience:** Low thousands in the US (~2,500 APRA members represent the core)
- **How they search:** "prospect research tools," "political giving data for nonprofits," "wealth screening alternatives"

The buyer and user often overlap — the Director of Prospect Development is frequently a former prospect researcher who still uses the tools daily.

#### Persona 2: The User — Prospect Research Analyst

This is who uses the tool every day.

**What they do:**
- Research potential donors to determine who has the capacity (money) and inclination (affinity) to make significant gifts
- Compile prospect profiles with wealth indicators, philanthropic history, affinity signals, and recommended ask amounts
- Screen large prospect lists against wealth/giving databases
- Deliver briefings to gift officers before donor meetings

**Their current workflow for political giving:**
1. Receive a research request or identify a prospect through screening
2. Manually search FEC.gov or OpenSecrets.org by name
3. Cross-reference with other data sources
4. Compile a prospect profile
5. Deliver to the gift officer

**Their pain points:**
- Manual FEC searches are tedious and don't scale (one name at a time)
- Name matching is poor — searching "Jonathan" doesn't find "Jon" or "Johnny"
- Existing tools (iWave, DonorSearch) have political giving modules that users describe as **"not very helpful"** — they find more data searching manually
- Noisy results for common names
- No batch processing against full FEC data
- Copy-pasting between FEC.gov and their CRM is time-consuming

**Tools they currently use:**
- CRM: Raiser's Edge NXT (dominant), Salesforce Nonprofit Cloud, DonorPerfect
- Wealth screening: DonorSearch, iWave/Kindsight, WealthEngine, Blackbaud ResearchPoint
- Manual: FEC.gov, OpenSecrets.org, FollowTheMoney.org, Google, LinkedIn, ProPublica

**Salary:** $65,000-$107,000/year (senior roles up to $160,000)

#### Persona 3: The Influencer — Major Gift Officer

Gift officers consume the output (prospect briefings, alerts) but don't choose the tool.

**What they do all day:**
- Manage a portfolio of 50-150 high-value prospects
- Donor meetings (breakfast, lunch, campus visits)
- Travel ~every third week to meet prospects in their territory
- Update CRM notes after every interaction
- Write thank-you notes
- Prepare for next day's meetings

**What they need from prospect research:**
- **Capacity estimate:** How much can this person give? (real estate, stocks, business, political giving)
- **Affinity signals:** Why would they give to *us*? (alumni status, volunteer history, values alignment)
- **Philanthropic history:** Have they given elsewhere? How much?
- **Timing:** Is there a recent event (stock sale, promotion, political donation) that makes this a good moment to call?

**How they use political giving data:**
- As one data point to calibrate ask amounts
- To understand donor values and interests
- To identify capacity that isn't visible through other signals
- To time outreach around giving activity

**Their biggest pain points:**
1. Time management — balancing research, meetings, travel, CRM data entry
2. Portfolio bloat — too many prospects, not enough time
3. Data gaps — incomplete prospect profiles, having to manually research
4. Missed opportunities — "We found out too late" about a wealth event
5. Turnover — average tenure is 18-24 months, institutional knowledge constantly lost

**Salary:** $53,900-$193,300/year (median ~$90K-$99K)

#### The Budget Holder — VP of Advancement

Sponsors the spend. Cares about "are we missing donor opportunities?" Approves the purchase.

## 2. Why They Care About Political Giving Data

### The Signal

Political giving is one of the strongest predictive signals for charitable giving capacity:

> **A prospect who has contributed at least $2,500 to political campaigns is almost 15x more likely to make a charitable donation than someone who has not.**

This is because political giving reveals:
1. **Capacity** — They have disposable income and are willing to give it away
2. **Intent** — They are habitual givers who act on their beliefs with money
3. **Values** — You know what causes they care about (education, healthcare, environment)
4. **Engagement** — They are active, not passive, with their wealth

### What It Tells Them That Other Data Doesn't

| Data Source | What It Reveals | Limitation |
|-------------|----------------|------------|
| Real estate | Wealth (asset value) | Doesn't show willingness to give |
| SEC filings | Liquidity event (stock sale) | Timing signal only |
| Foundation 990s | Board service + giving | Only covers foundation donors |
| **Political giving** | **Capacity + willingness + values + engagement** | **All four signals in one data point** |

### The "We Found Out Too Late" Fear

Every prospect researcher has a story about a major gift opportunity they missed because nobody was watching. The cost of missing one $5M gift opportunity dwarfs years of subscription cost.

This same FOMO drives purchases of news monitoring tools (like Alma News) and wealth screening platforms. Political giving data feeds the same need: "I need to know about my prospects' financial activity before my competitor institution does."

## 3. The Legal Landscape (Critical)

### The FEC Restriction

Under the **Federal Election Campaign Act (52 U.S.C. 30111(a)(4))**, information about individual contributors from FEC reports may not be sold or used:
- For **soliciting contributions** (including charitable contributions)
- For any **commercial purpose**

### The 2018 Clarification That Changed Everything

In **November 2018**, the FEC clarified that "soliciting contributions" includes soliciting **any type** of contribution or donation — including **charitable** contributions. This was not previously clear and was a watershed moment for the prospect research field.

### Enforcement

- **May 2021:** FEC ruled that Tally Up could not use aggregate individual contribution data in analytics to help campaigns target likely donors
- **October 2021:** FEC settled with Rep. Ro Khanna, fining him **$16,000** for using software that employed FEC individual contribution data to score potential donors

### APRA's Position (The Professional Association)

**APRA (Association of Professional Researchers for Advancement)** — the leading professional organization for prospect researchers with 2,500+ members — officially advises:

> "No FEC data is to be used in any facet of prospect development work."

The **GOBEL Group** published a white paper recommending that nonprofit organizations avoid factoring individual political giving into ratings, research, or solicitation plans due to legal exposure risk.

### The Messy Reality

Despite the official guidance:
- **DonorSearch, iWave, and WealthEngine still include FEC data** in their platforms with disclaimers
- Many organizations continue using political giving data while monitoring the legal landscape
- iWave explicitly notes FEC Act compliance — they provide political data for "context" only, not for scoring or solicitation
- DonorSearch offers an option to **hide** political giving from view (configurable per organization)
- There is active professional debate: some practitioners believe looking up a known donor on FEC.gov is different from bulk-screening a database

### What This Means for Our Product

**We must navigate this carefully:**

1. **Position as informational/research context**, not a solicitation tool
2. **Never market it as a way to identify who to solicit** — frame it as awareness/intelligence
3. **Include clear disclaimers** about FEC data usage restrictions
4. **Offer the ability to opt out** of political data (like DonorSearch does)
5. **Follow iWave's model:** provide data for context, not for scoring or matching algorithms that drive solicitation decisions
6. **Consult a US attorney** before commercial launch

**The market reality:** Despite APRA's guidance, the demand exists. DonorSearch has 13,000+ customers. iWave has thousands more. Both include FEC data. The question is not whether the market wants this data — it does — but how to provide it responsibly.

## 4. Competitive Landscape

### The Incumbent Stack

| Tool | Price/yr | Political Giving | Strengths | Weaknesses |
|------|----------|-----------------|-----------|------------|
| **DonorSearch** | ~$1,200-$5,000 | Included (can hide) | Largest philanthropic database (240M+ gifts), 13K+ customers, good entry-level option | Shallow profiling, data freshness issues, aging UI |
| **iWave / Kindsight** | ~$3,545-$4,150+ | Included (FEC-compliant, weak) | Best user satisfaction (96/100, #1 for 16 quarters), 360-degree profiles | Token-based pricing frustrating, **political giving module acknowledged as weak by users**, expensive |
| **WealthEngine** | ~$5,000-$50,000 | Minimal | Deepest wealth/asset data, predictive modeling | Very expensive, data accuracy complaints, overkill for small orgs |
| **Windfall** | Custom | None | Precise net worth estimates, weekly refreshes | No giving history, no affinity scoring, $1M+ threshold only |
| **Blackbaud ResearchPoint** | Bundled w/ RE | Not prominent | Integrated with Raiser's Edge ecosystem | Closed ecosystem, expensive, less flexible |
| **EverTrue** | Custom | Via DonorSearch | Strong for higher ed, mobile-friendly | Costly, depends on DonorSearch for political data |
| **GivingDNA** | Custom | "Political leanings" | Analytics + screening in one, unlimited screenings | Unclear depth on political data |
| **Nexis for Dev** | Custom | Public records | Deepest public records access (81B records) | Not specialized for advancement |

### The Real #1 Competitor: Manual Search

Most prospect researchers who want political giving data do this:
1. Go to **FEC.gov/data** or **OpenSecrets.org**
2. Type in a prospect's name
3. Scroll through results trying to find the right person
4. Copy the data into their CRM or spreadsheet
5. Repeat for the next prospect

This takes 5-10 minutes per prospect. At 10,000 prospects, that's 800-1,600 hours of manual work. Most teams give up after their top 50-200 prospects.

### Where Every Competitor Falls Short on Political Giving

Both research agents confirmed the same gap from different angles:

1. **iWave users explicitly say the political giving module is "not very helpful"** — they find more data by searching manually on other resources
2. **No tool does batch matching** of an entire prospect list against full FEC bulk data
3. **No tool provides deep political giving analysis** — trend analysis over time, employer cross-referencing, partisan lean scoring, committee-type classification
4. **Name variant matching is poor** — iWave users complain that searching "Jonathan" doesn't return "Jon" or "Johnny"
5. **No tool specializes in political giving** — they all treat it as a minor checkbox feature within broader wealth screening

### The Gap We Fill

| Capability | Existing Tools | Our Tool |
|-----------|---------------|----------|
| FEC batch matching (full prospect list) | No (profile-by-profile lookup) | Yes (entire list vs FEC data) |
| Name variant matching | Poor ("Jonathan" misses "Jon") | Advanced (93 nickname groups, suffix handling) |
| Employer cross-referencing | No | Yes (prospect company vs FEC employer) |
| Partisan lean analysis | No | Yes (extrapolated from giving pattern) |
| Individual donation detail | Partial (year + range + candidate) | Full (every donation, amount, date, recipient) |
| Committee/candidate enrichment | Minimal | Full (resolve CMTE_ID to candidate name, party, office) |
| Multi-source (federal + state + 527 + lobbying) | Federal only (some include state) | All four sources |
| Price | $3,500-$15,000+/yr | TBD (positioned much lower) |

- **Salesforce Nonprofit Cloud:** 54,000+ organizations adopted
- **DonorSearch:** 13,000+ customers

### Our Addressable Slice

We're not competing for the full $2.38B market. Our slice is:
- Organizations that **already do prospect research** (5,000-10,000)
- Who want **political giving data specifically** (subset — maybe 2,000-5,000)
- At a **price point below DonorSearch** ($500-$1,500/yr)

Even at the low end: 500 customers × $1,000/yr = $500K ARR. That's the floor if the product works.

### Professional Associations

| Association | What They Do | Relevance |
|-------------|-------------|-----------|
| **APRA** (Association of Professional Researchers for Advancement) | Professional home for prospect researchers. 2,500+ members, 29 regional chapters, annual conference. | The core community. Their ethical guidance on FEC data matters enormously. |

| **AFP** (Association of Fundraising Professionals) | General fundraising professionals. | Less relevant for prospect research tools specifically. |

### The PRSPCT-L Listserv

The primary online community for prospect researchers. Highly influential. Word-of-mouth recommendations here drive tool adoption. Getting discussed positively on PRSPCT-L would be more valuable than any ad campaign.

### Typical Tech Stack at a Target Institution

```
CRM:                Raiser's Edge NXT or Salesforce Nonprofit Cloud
Wealth Screening:   DonorSearch or iWave (sometimes both)
News Monitoring:    Google Alerts (manual) or Alma News or Insightful Philanthropy
Political Giving:   Manual FEC.gov search (or whatever DonorSearch/iWave provides)
General Research:   LinkedIn, Google, ProPublica 990 Finder, state records
```

Our tool slots into the **Political Giving** row — either replacing manual FEC.gov searches or supplementing the weak political giving modules in DonorSearch/iWave.

### Industry Trends Working in Our Favor

1. **Fewer donors, larger gifts** — major donor identification is more critical than ever
2. **AI-enhanced analytics growing fastest** — institutions are adopting data tools
3. **Vendor consolidation** — Anthology bankruptcy (Sept 2025) is creating market disruption
4. **36% of nonprofits ended 2024 in deficit** — budget pressure means cheaper specialized tools over expensive all-in-one platforms
5. **Total charitable giving hit $592.5B** — the pie is growing even as donor counts shrink

## 7. How to Position This Product

### For Prospect Researchers (Users)

> "Stop manually searching FEC.gov one name at a time. Upload your entire prospect list — get back every political donation, matched and enriched, in minutes."

**Value prop:** Scale (10,000+ names vs 50 manual searches), accuracy (name variant matching that actually works), depth (every donation with full detail, not just year + range).

### For Directors of Prospect Development (Buyers)

> "Your team wastes hours manually searching FEC.gov and OpenSecrets. Your iWave political giving module is 'not very helpful' (their users' words, not ours). This tool does one thing exceptionally well: matches your entire prospect list against comprehensive political contribution data — federal, state, 527, and lobbying — and delivers a client-ready CSV."

### For VPs of Advancement (Budget Holders)

> "A prospect who gives $2,500+ politically is 15x more likely to make a charitable gift. This tool identifies those prospects in your database — the ones your current tools are missing."

### Positioning Relative to Competitors

**Not a replacement for DonorSearch or iWave.** Those are broad wealth screening platforms. We're a specialized add-on that fills their acknowledged gap in political giving data.

**The analogy:** DonorSearch is a Swiss Army knife. We're a surgical scalpel — for one specific cut, we're dramatically better.

### Pricing Strategy

The market is dominated by $3,500-$15,000/yr platforms. Position below them:
- **$500-$1,500/yr** for a specialized political giving tool
- Complementary, not competitive — "use alongside your existing wealth screening"
- The ROI argument: one identified major gift prospect pays for years of subscription

### Compliance Positioning

Follow iWave's proven model:
- Provide political giving data **for informational context only**
- Do not use FEC data in scoring algorithms
- Include clear compliance disclaimers
- Offer ability to hide/remove political data per organization
- Position as a research tool, not a solicitation tool
